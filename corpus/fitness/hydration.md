# Hydration for Training

General information, not medical advice. Certain conditions (kidney or heart issues) and some medications affect fluid needs — follow your doctor's guidance if that applies to you.

## Why it matters

Water makes up most of your muscle and is involved in nearly every bodily process, including temperature regulation and moving nutrients around. Even **mild dehydration — around 2% of body weight** — can reduce strength and endurance, blunt concentration, and make a normal workout feel harder than it should.

## How much do you need?

There's no single magic number; needs vary with body size, climate, and activity. Common general guidance lands around **2 to 3 liters (roughly 8–12 cups) of total fluid per day** for adults, from drinks *and* food. You need more when it's hot or when you sweat heavily during training.

A practical, low-effort gauge is **urine color**: pale straw usually means well-hydrated; dark yellow suggests you should drink more. Thirst is a decent signal too, though it lags a little behind actual need.

## Around your workout

- **Before:** arrive already hydrated; a glass or two of water in the hour beforehand is plenty for most sessions.
- **During:** sip as needed. For typical gym sessions under about an hour, water is all you need.
- **After:** drink to replace what you lost, especially if you sweated a lot.

## Do you need sports drinks or electrolytes?

For most everyday gym sessions, **plain water is enough**. Electrolyte or carbohydrate drinks are mainly useful for prolonged (roughly 60–90+ minutes), intense, or very sweaty exercise, or in hot conditions where you're losing a lot of salt. Everyday hydration is also supported by a normal diet, which contains plenty of sodium and potassium.

## Can you drink too much?

Rarely, but yes — drinking excessive water in a short time can dangerously dilute blood sodium (hyponatremia). This is uncommon in normal gym use; you'd have to force large volumes well beyond thirst. The goal is *adequate*, steady hydration, not maximal.

## The takeaway

Drink regularly through the day, use urine color and thirst as guides, keep water handy during training, and reach for electrolytes only for long or very sweaty sessions. Good hydration is a cheap, easy way to protect how you feel and perform.
