# Workout Programming Basics

General educational information for beginners and returning lifters. It is not a medical or individualized coaching plan; get clearance from a doctor before beginning a new program if you have health concerns.

## What "programming" means

Programming is just the structure behind your training: how often you train, which movements you do, how many sets and reps, how much weight, and how it all changes over time. A good beginner program is simple, repeatable, and progresses gradually.

## Sets, reps, and load

- A **rep** (repetition) is one complete movement. A **set** is a group of reps done back-to-back.
- **Load** is the weight used.
- These trade off against each other: heavier loads allow fewer reps; lighter loads allow more.

Common general-purpose ranges:

- **Strength-leaning:** ~3–6 reps per set at heavier loads.
- **Muscle-building ("hypertrophy"):** ~6–15 reps.
- **Endurance-leaning:** ~15+ reps at lighter loads.

Most beginners do well anywhere in roughly the **8–15 rep** range on most exercises — it builds muscle, is forgiving on form, and is easy on the joints.

## How many sets and how often

- **Sets:** a common starting point is **2–4 working sets per exercise**. Total weekly sets per muscle group in the ballpark of **~10** is a frequently cited target for growth, but beginners see progress on far less.
- **Frequency:** training each muscle group about **2 times per week** tends to work better than once, but even 2–3 full-body sessions a week is plenty to start.

## Choosing exercises

Cover the major movement patterns across the week:

1. A **squat/leg press** pattern (quads, glutes).
2. A **hip-hinge or leg-curl** pattern (hamstrings, glutes).
3. A **horizontal push** (chest press, push-up).
4. A **horizontal pull** (row).
5. A **vertical push** (overhead/shoulder press).
6. A **vertical pull** (lat pulldown, assisted pull-up).
7. Some **core** work.

Machines are a great starting point: they guide the movement path, so you can focus on effort instead of balance, which is why beginner-oriented apps and gyms lean on them.

## Full-body vs. split routines

- **Full-body** (each session hits most of the body) suits beginners and people training 2–3 times a week. It's forgiving if you miss a day.
- **Splits** (e.g. upper/lower, or push/pull/legs) divide the body across days and suit people training 4+ times a week who want more volume per muscle.

Neither is "better" in the abstract — match it to how many days you'll realistically train.

## Warm-up, work, cool-down

Each session generally has three parts: a brief general warm-up plus a light ramp-up set on your first exercise, your working sets, and an optional easy cool-down. (See the warm-up and mobility document.)

## Rest between sets

Rest long enough to do the next set with good form. Rough guides: **~1–2 minutes** for moderate-rep work, **2–3+ minutes** for heavy low-rep strength work, shorter for light endurance work.

## Progression is the engine

A program that never gets harder stops working. Over weeks you gradually add reps, sets, or weight — this is **progressive overload**, covered in its own document. Small, steady increases beat big jumps.

## Deloads and recovery

Every several weeks, or when you feel run-down, an easier **deload** week (lighter weights or fewer sets) helps you recover and come back fresh. Recovery — sleep, food, rest days — is when adaptation actually happens.

## Keep it sustainable

The best program is the one you'll actually follow. Start easy, leave sessions feeling like you could have done a little more, and let the difficulty creep up over months. Consistency is the real variable that matters.
