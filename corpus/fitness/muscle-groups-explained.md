# The Major Muscle Groups, Explained Simply

General educational information to help you understand what you're training and why. Not medical advice.

## Why bother knowing this

You don't need anatomy expertise to train well, but a rough map helps you build balanced sessions, understand what an exercise works, and spot gaps (like always training the front of the body and neglecting the back).

## The big regions

### Legs
The largest muscles you have, so training them does the most for overall strength and everyday function.
- **Quadriceps (quads)** — front of the thigh; straighten the knee. Trained by leg presses and leg extensions.
- **Hamstrings** — back of the thigh; bend the knee and extend the hip. Trained by leg curls and hinge movements.
- **Glutes** — the hips/buttocks; the powerhouse for standing, climbing, and hip extension.
- **Calves** — lower leg; help with walking and pushing off.
- **Inner and outer hips (adductors/abductors)** — stabilize the pelvis and knees with every step.

### Chest
- **Pectorals (pecs)** — the pushing muscles at the front of the torso. Trained by chest presses, pec flys, and push-ups. Pushing strength is what you use getting up off the floor or pushing a heavy door.

### Back
- **Lats (latissimus dorsi)** — the broad muscles down the sides of the back; pull the arms down and in. Trained by pulldowns, pull-ups, and rows.
- **Rhomboids and mid-traps** — between the shoulder blades; pull them together for good posture. Trained by rows.
- **Erectors (lower back)** — run along the spine; keep you upright and protect the back when you bend and lift.

### Shoulders
- **Deltoids (delts)** — cap the shoulder, with front, side, and rear portions. Overhead presses hit the front/side; rear-delt work (often neglected) helps posture.

### Arms
- **Biceps** — front of the upper arm; bend the elbow (carrying, lifting).
- **Triceps** — back of the upper arm; straighten the elbow, and do most of the work in any push.

### Core
- **Abdominals** — front of the midsection; a steady core takes load off the lower back in everything you do.
- **Obliques** — sides of the waist; handle twisting and steady you when you reach.

## Push vs. pull

A handy way to keep sessions balanced:
- **Push** movements (chest press, shoulder press, triceps) work the front and pressing muscles.
- **Pull** movements (rows, pulldowns, biceps, rear delts) work the back and pulling muscles.

Desk life tends to over-develop the front and weaken the back, so many people benefit from emphasizing pulling and rear-shoulder work for better posture.

## Building a balanced week

Across your training week, aim to hit: a leg push, a leg curl/hinge, a horizontal push, a horizontal pull, a vertical push, a vertical pull, and some core. That coverage keeps you strong and balanced without needing to think about individual muscles session to session.
