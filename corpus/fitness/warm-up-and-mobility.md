# Warming Up and Mobility

General educational information. If you have a specific injury or condition, get individualized guidance from a doctor or physical therapist before following general mobility advice.

## Why warm up

A warm-up gradually raises your heart rate, body temperature, and blood flow to the muscles, and mentally shifts you into training. Warmer muscles and joints move more freely and are more comfortable to load. It doesn't need to be long — a few focused minutes is enough for most gym sessions.

## A simple warm-up routine

1. **5–8 minutes of easy cardio.** A treadmill walk, stationary bike, rower, or cross-trainer — just enough to feel a little warm and slightly out of breath. This is the general warm-up.
2. **A few dynamic movements** (optional but helpful). Gentle, controlled motions through a comfortable range — arm circles, leg swings, bodyweight squats, torso rotations. These are "dynamic stretches," done with movement rather than holding a position.
3. **One or two light ramp-up sets on your first exercise.** Before your working weight, do a set with a light load to groove the movement pattern. This is the most exercise-specific and useful part of the warm-up.

That's it. You don't need to warm up every machine from scratch — one light set on the first exercise of a session, plus the general warm-up, covers most people.

## Static stretching: when?

Holding a stretch for 20–60 seconds ("static stretching") is best saved for **after** training or for separate mobility sessions. Long static stretches immediately before heavy lifting can briefly reduce power output, so keep pre-workout stretching dynamic and light.

## Mobility vs. flexibility

- **Flexibility** is how far a muscle can lengthen (passive range).
- **Mobility** is how well you can actively control a joint through its range.

For most gym-goers, training through a full, controlled range of motion on your exercises already builds useful mobility. Dedicated mobility work is most valuable where you feel restricted — commonly the hips, ankles, upper back, and shoulders.

## A few gentle mobility moves

- **Hips:** deep bodyweight squat holds, hip circles.
- **Ankles:** controlled forward knee rocks over the toes.
- **Upper back / shoulders:** thoracic rotations, band pull-aparts, slow shoulder circles.

Keep everything pain-free and controlled — mobility work should feel like easing into range, never forcing it.

## The takeaway

Spend a few minutes warming up before you lift: a little easy cardio, some dynamic movement, and a light ramp-up set. Save long static stretches for afterward. Address mobility where you actually feel tight, and never force a joint into pain.
