# Strength Training vs. Cardio: Do You Need Both?

General information, not a personalized program. Check with a doctor before starting new exercise if you have heart, lung, or joint concerns.

## They do different jobs

**Strength (resistance) training** — lifting weights, using machines, or bodyweight exercises — builds and maintains muscle and bone, improves the force your muscles can produce, and raises your resting metabolism slightly by adding lean tissue. It's the main driver of getting *stronger* and of holding onto muscle as you age.

**Cardiovascular ("cardio") training** — brisk walking, cycling, swimming, rowing, running — trains your heart, lungs, and circulation. It improves aerobic capacity (endurance), supports heart health, and burns a meaningful number of calories during the activity itself.

Neither replaces the other. They target largely different adaptations.

## What the general guidelines suggest

Widely cited public-health guidance for adults recommends, roughly:

- **At least 150 minutes per week of moderate-intensity aerobic activity** (or 75 minutes vigorous), and
- **Muscle-strengthening activities on 2 or more days per week**, covering the major muscle groups.

So the common recommendation isn't "pick one" — it's a mix, weighted toward whatever matches your goals.

## Matching it to your goal

- **Get stronger / build muscle:** make strength training the priority. Keep some cardio for heart health and recovery, but don't let it crowd out lifting.
- **Improve endurance / heart health:** emphasize cardio, but keep 2 strength sessions a week to protect muscle and joints.
- **General health and feeling better day-to-day:** a balance of both, plus daily movement (walking counts).
- **Fat loss:** driven mostly by diet (calorie balance). Strength training preserves muscle during a deficit; cardio adds to calorie expenditure. Both help.

## Can you do them together?

Yes. Many people combine them in a single session or split them across days. A common, simple approach: a short cardio warm-up, then strength work, then optional extra cardio at the end. If getting stronger is the goal and you're doing hard cardio and lifting in the same session, doing the strength work first (when you're fresh) is usually preferred.

## The "interference" question

Very high volumes of endurance training done alongside strength training can modestly blunt strength/muscle gains — the so-called interference effect. For a typical beginner or general trainee doing moderate amounts of each, this is rarely a practical problem. It mainly matters for athletes pushing both to extremes.

## Bottom line

For most people, some of each is the sweet spot: strength training at least twice a week to build and keep muscle and bone, plus regular aerobic activity for your heart and endurance. Weight the balance toward your primary goal, and remember that consistency over months matters more than the exact ratio.
