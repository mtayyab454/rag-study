# Common Exercise Form Cues (Q&A)

General technique guidance for common machine and beginner exercises. This is educational, not a substitute for hands-on coaching. If a movement causes pain (not normal effort), stop and consider seeing a professional.

**Q: What's the single most important form principle?**
A: Control the weight through a full, comfortable range of motion — smooth on the way up and, especially, slow and controlled on the way down. Momentum and jerky reps do less for you and raise injury risk. Quality reps beat heavy sloppy ones.

**Q: How should I breathe?**
A: A simple general rule is to exhale during the harder (lifting) phase and inhale during the easier (lowering) phase. The main thing is: don't hold your breath and strain for long periods. Just keep breathing.

**Q: What are good cues for a leg press?**
A: Push through your heels, keep your lower back flat against the pad, and stop before your knees lock out at the top. Don't let your knees cave inward, and don't bring them so far toward your chest that your hips round off the seat.

**Q: What about a chest press or push-up?**
A: Keep your back supported (on the machine) or your body in a straight line (push-up), press smoothly without snapping your elbows straight, and don't let the movement pinch the front of your shoulders. Bring the handles or your chest to a comfortable depth, not an extreme one.

**Q: How do I row or do a lat pulldown correctly?**
A: Sit tall and lead with your elbows, pulling toward your torso; think about drawing your shoulder blades back and down. For a lat pulldown, pull the bar to your upper chest — **not** behind your neck — and let it rise back up under control.

**Q: Overhead / shoulder press cues?**
A: Keep your core braced and back supported, press straight up, and stop just short of fully locking the elbows. If reaching all the way overhead pinches, shorten the range to where it's comfortable.

**Q: Any cues for squats (bodyweight or barbell)?**
A: Feet about shoulder-width, toes slightly out, sit back and down as if to a chair, keep your chest up and knees tracking over your toes, and go only as deep as you can while keeping a neutral (not rounded) lower back. Drive up through your whole foot.

**Q: How do I protect my lower back on hinging movements (rows, back extensions, deadlift-style lifts)?**
A: Keep a neutral spine — neither rounded nor over-arched — hinge at the hips, and avoid yanking or leaning back to move the weight. On a back extension, rise only until your body is straight; don't arch past it.

**Q: How do I know if the weight is too heavy?**
A: If you can't complete your reps with the same clean technique you used on the first rep, or you have to swing/cheat the weight up, it's too heavy. Drop the load and keep the form honest.

**Q: Soreness vs. pain — what's the difference?**
A: General muscle soreness a day or two later is normal, especially when new to an exercise. Sharp, pinching, or joint pain during a movement is a signal to stop, reduce range or load, and — if it persists — get it looked at by a professional.

**Q: Do machines have simpler form than free weights?**
A: Generally yes — a machine guides the movement path, so you mostly have to set the seat correctly, control the weight, and use a comfortable range. That's why they're a friendly starting point. Still apply the same principles: full range, controlled tempo, no pain.
