# Common Beginner Mistakes (and How to Avoid Them)

General educational information for people new to the gym. If you're unsure whether exercise is safe for you, get clearance from a doctor first.

Most beginner setbacks come from a handful of avoidable habits. Here are the common ones.

## 1. Doing too much, too soon

The most common mistake. Enthusiasm leads to long sessions, too many exercises, or too much weight in week one — which causes excessive soreness, burnout, or injury. **Fix:** start with short, manageable sessions and leave feeling like you could have done a bit more. Let volume and intensity creep up over weeks.

## 2. Adding weight before earning it

Ego-lifting — piling on weight you can't control — wrecks form and invites injury. **Fix:** only increase the load when you can complete all your reps with clean technique. Progress in small steps.

## 3. Sacrificing form for numbers

Half reps, momentum, and jerky movements move more weight but train you less and hurt more. **Fix:** prioritize a controlled full range of motion. Clean reps at a lighter weight beat sloppy reps at a heavier one.

## 4. Skipping the warm-up

Jumping straight into working sets cold makes the first sets feel worse and is less comfortable on the joints. **Fix:** a few minutes of easy cardio plus a light ramp-up set on your first exercise.

## 5. Neglecting recovery and sleep

Training hard while under-sleeping and under-eating stalls progress. Muscle is built during recovery, not during the workout. **Fix:** aim for 7–9 hours of sleep, eat enough protein and total calories, and take rest days.

## 6. Program-hopping

Changing programs every week chasing the "perfect" routine means you never stay on anything long enough to progress. **Fix:** pick a simple, sensible plan and run it consistently for a couple of months before judging it.

## 7. Under-eating protein

Not eating enough protein slows recovery and muscle gain. **Fix:** aim for a protein target spread across the day (see the protein document).

## 8. Ignoring the lower body or pulling muscles

Beginners often over-train the "mirror muscles" (chest, arms) and skip legs, back, and posterior work. **Fix:** cover all the major movement patterns across the week — push, pull, legs, and core.

## 9. Comparing yourself to others

Everyone starts somewhere, and the strongest person in the room was once new. Comparison breeds discouragement or reckless ego-lifting. **Fix:** measure progress against your *own* past sessions.

## 10. Expecting fast results

Visible change takes weeks to months, and progress isn't linear. Quitting at week three is the real failure mode. **Fix:** commit to consistency and judge results over months, not days. Showing up is most of the battle.

## The theme

Almost every mistake above resolves to the same advice: **start easy, keep your form honest, recover well, and be patient.** Do that and the results take care of themselves.
