# Recovery and Sleep

General information for gym-goers. Persistent fatigue, pain, or sleep problems warrant a conversation with a doctor.

## Training breaks you down; recovery builds you up

Exercise is the *stimulus* for getting stronger, but the actual adaptation — repairing muscle, restocking energy, strengthening tissue — happens during **recovery**, mostly while you rest and sleep. Train without recovering and you accumulate fatigue faster than you adapt, which stalls progress and raises injury risk.

## Sleep is the foundation

Sleep is the single most important recovery tool, and the one people neglect most.

- **Aim for roughly 7–9 hours** per night for most adults.
- Deep sleep is when much of the body's tissue repair and hormone release (including growth hormone) is concentrated.
- Short-changing sleep reduces strength, coordination, and motivation, increases perceived effort, and can nudge appetite hormones toward overeating.

Simple sleep hygiene helps: keep a consistent bedtime, dim lights and screens before bed, keep the room cool and dark, and go easy on caffeine in the afternoon and alcohol in the evening.

## Rest days and muscle groups

You don't need to be sore to have trained well, and you don't need to train every day. Giving a muscle group roughly **a day or two** before hammering it hard again is a reasonable general guideline. Full-body routines naturally build in this spacing; splits rotate which muscles work each day.

Active recovery — an easy walk, light cycling, gentle mobility — on rest days can help you feel better without adding meaningful fatigue.

## Soreness (DOMS)

Delayed-onset muscle soreness typically shows up 12–48 hours after unfamiliar or harder-than-usual training and fades within a few days. It's normal, especially when starting out or trying new exercises, and it isn't a reliable measure of a good workout. Sharp or joint pain is different from muscle soreness and shouldn't be pushed through.

## Signs you may need more recovery

- Performance going backwards over several sessions.
- Persistent heavy fatigue, poor sleep, or low mood.
- Nagging aches that don't settle.
- Loss of motivation to train.

The usual fix is more sleep, a lighter week (a deload), and adequate food — not more effort.

## Food and hydration are recovery too

Eating enough total calories and protein, and staying hydrated, are part of recovery, not separate from it. See the nutrition, protein, and hydration documents.

## The takeaway

Treat sleep and rest as part of the program, not an afterthought. Consistent 7–9 hour nights, sensible spacing between hard sessions, and easier weeks when you're run-down will do more for your progress than adding extra workouts.
