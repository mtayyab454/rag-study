# Nutrition and Macronutrients: A General Guide

This is general information for gym-goers, not personalized nutrition or medical advice. Individual needs vary; consult a doctor or registered dietitian before making significant dietary changes, especially if you have a health condition.

## The three macronutrients

Almost everything you eat is built from three **macronutrients** — protein, carbohydrate, and fat — plus water, fiber, vitamins, and minerals. Each macro provides energy, measured in calories (kcal):

- **Protein** — about 4 kcal per gram.
- **Carbohydrate** — about 4 kcal per gram.
- **Fat** — about 9 kcal per gram.

Alcohol, for completeness, provides about 7 kcal per gram but isn't a nutrient your body needs.

## What each macro does

### Protein
Protein supplies the amino acids your body uses to repair and build tissue, including muscle. For people doing resistance training, adequate protein is the single most important dietary factor for recovery and gaining strength. It's also the most satiating macro, which helps with appetite.

### Carbohydrate
Carbs are your body's preferred quick fuel, especially for higher-intensity training. They're stored in muscles and the liver as glycogen. "Complex" carbs (whole grains, legumes, vegetables, fruit) come packaged with fiber and micronutrients; heavily refined carbs and added sugars provide energy with little else.

### Fat
Dietary fat supports hormone production, helps absorb fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K), and cushions organs. Unsaturated fats (olive oil, nuts, avocado, fatty fish) are generally emphasized over large amounts of saturated fat.

## Calorie balance sets the direction

Whether you lose, maintain, or gain weight is governed largely by **energy balance** — calories in versus calories out over time:

- **Deficit** (fewer calories than you burn) → fat loss.
- **Maintenance** → weight stays roughly stable.
- **Surplus** (more than you burn) → weight gain, which for a trainee is a mix of muscle and fat.

Macros then shape the *quality* of that change. For example, keeping protein high while in a deficit helps preserve muscle.

## A rough starting split

There's no single "correct" macro split, but a common general-purpose starting point for an active adult looks like the table below. Treat these as illustrative ranges, not prescriptions.

| Macro | Common range (% of calories) | Rough grams for a 2,000 kcal day |
|---|---|---|
| Protein | 25–35% | 125–175 g |
| Carbohydrate | 35–50% | 175–250 g |
| Fat | 20–35% | 44–78 g |

Protein is often set first by body weight (see the dedicated protein document), with carbs and fat filling the remaining calories according to preference and training demands.

## Micronutrients and fiber still matter

Hitting your macros while eating only ultra-processed food will leave gaps. Aim for a foundation of whole foods — plenty of vegetables and fruit, lean protein sources, whole grains, and healthy fats — so you also get fiber (helpful for digestion and fullness) and the vitamins and minerals that macro counting ignores. A general target of around 25–38 g of fiber per day is commonly cited for adults.

## Timing is secondary

Total daily intake matters far more than precise meal timing for most people. Getting protein spread reasonably across the day and eating enough carbohydrate around harder training sessions can help, but you don't need to obsess over "anabolic windows." Consistency across weeks beats perfection on any single day.

## Estimating your calorie needs

Before splitting macros, it helps to have a rough idea of how many calories you burn in a day — your **total daily energy expenditure (TDEE)**. It has a few parts:

- **Basal metabolic rate (BMR):** the energy your body uses at complete rest — typically the largest chunk, often 60–70% of the total.
- **Activity:** everything you do, from deliberate exercise to fidgeting and walking around (the latter is sometimes called NEAT, non-exercise activity thermogenesis).
- **The thermic effect of food:** the energy used digesting what you eat — protein has the highest thermic effect, another reason it's useful.

You don't need a precise number. A common practical approach is to estimate a starting calorie target, eat at it consistently for two to three weeks, track your body weight trend, and adjust up or down based on what actually happens on the scale and in the mirror. Real-world feedback beats any formula.

## Adjusting for your goal

- **Fat loss:** a **modest** deficit — often cited as around 300–500 calories per day below maintenance — tends to be sustainable and preserves more muscle than aggressive crash dieting. Keep protein high and keep lifting.
- **Muscle gain:** a **small** surplus — often around 200–400 calories per day above maintenance — supports building muscle while limiting excess fat gain. A larger surplus mostly adds fat faster, not muscle faster.
- **Maintenance / recomposition:** eating around maintenance with high protein and consistent training can slowly build muscle while losing a little fat, especially for beginners and people returning after a break.

Rates matter: aiming for roughly 0.5–1% of body weight change per week (in either direction) is a common, sustainable guideline. Faster isn't better.

## Reading a food label

A nutrition label gives you everything you need to fit a food into your macros: the **serving size**, the **calories per serving**, and the grams of **protein, carbohydrate (including sugars and fiber), and fat**. Two quick habits make labels far more useful:

1. **Check the serving size first.** The numbers are per serving, and packages often contain several servings — it's easy to eat three servings while reading numbers for one.
2. **Sanity-check the calories against the macros.** Multiply protein and carbs by 4 and fat by 9; the total should land near the stated calories. This helps you catch mislabeled or misread values.

## Do you have to count macros?

No. Counting is a useful *tool*, not a requirement. Plenty of people reach their goals by building consistent habits instead — a palm-sized protein source at each meal, filling half the plate with vegetables, choosing mostly whole foods, and managing portions of calorie-dense items. Tracking is most helpful when you're stuck and want to know exactly what you're eating, or when you have a specific, time-bound goal. If tracking becomes stressful or obsessive, stepping back to simple habits is perfectly valid.

## Common pitfalls

- **Liquid calories add up fast.** Sugary drinks, large coffees, juice, and alcohol can quietly contribute a big share of a day's calories with little fullness in return.
- **Underestimating portions.** Cooking oils, dressings, nut butters, and "just a handful" snacks are dense and easy to under-count.
- **All-or-nothing thinking.** One off-plan meal doesn't undo a week. Consistency across weeks is what shapes results, not perfection on any single day.
- **Chasing "clean" labels over calories.** Foods marketed as healthy can still be calorie-dense; total intake still governs weight change.

## Hydration counts too

Water isn't a macronutrient but it's essential — see the dedicated hydration document. Even mild dehydration can dent training performance and make you feel more tired than you are.

## The bottom line

Eat enough total calories for your goal, prioritize protein, build meals around mostly whole foods, and stay consistent. The fine details of macro ratios matter far less than the basics done reliably. If you have specific health concerns or aggressive goals, work with a qualified professional.
