# How Much Protein Do You Need?

General information for people who train. It isn't medical advice — if you have kidney disease or another condition affecting protein tolerance, talk to your doctor first.

## The short answer

For adults doing regular resistance training, research commonly supports roughly **1.6 to 2.2 grams of protein per kilogram of body weight per day** (about **0.7 to 1.0 grams per pound**). Sedentary adults need less — the baseline recommended dietary allowance is around 0.8 g/kg — but that's a floor to prevent deficiency, not an optimum for someone lifting weights.

## Putting a number on it

For a 70 kg (154 lb) person training regularly:

- Lower end: 70 × 1.6 ≈ **112 g/day**
- Upper end: 70 × 2.2 ≈ **154 g/day**

Going well above ~2.2 g/kg generally offers little added benefit for muscle for most people, though it isn't harmful for healthy individuals.

## Why protein matters for training

- **Muscle repair and growth.** Training creates small amounts of muscle damage; protein supplies the amino acids to rebuild stronger.
- **Preserving muscle in a calorie deficit.** Higher protein helps you keep muscle while losing fat.
- **Satiety.** Protein is filling, which makes appetite easier to manage.

## Quality and sources

Complete proteins contain all the essential amino acids. Animal sources (eggs, dairy, meat, poultry, fish) are complete. Many plant sources are lower in one or more amino acids, but eating a **variety** of plant proteins across the day (legumes, soy, grains, nuts, seeds) covers the bases well. Soy, quinoa, and buckwheat are notable complete plant proteins.

Rough protein per common serving:

- 1 large egg ≈ 6 g
- 100 g cooked chicken breast ≈ 30 g
- 1 cup (about 245 g) Greek yogurt ≈ 20–23 g
- 100 g firm tofu ≈ 15–17 g
- 1 cup cooked lentils ≈ 18 g
- 1 scoop whey protein powder ≈ 20–25 g

## Spreading it out

Muscle protein synthesis responds to protein at each meal, so most guidance suggests **spreading intake across 3–4 meals** of roughly 20–40 g rather than eating it all at once. That said, hitting your **total** for the day is the priority; distribution is a smaller refinement.

## Do you need supplements?

No — protein powder is just a convenient food, not a requirement. If you can hit your target from whole foods, that's completely fine. Powders help when you struggle to eat enough or need something quick.

## A caution

Very high protein intakes are safe for healthy people but can be a concern for those with existing kidney disease. Anyone with a relevant medical condition should get individualized advice from a doctor or dietitian.
